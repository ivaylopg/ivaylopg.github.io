What I Do.

Lately I’ve had a hard time coming up with a short answer for “what do you do?” The following is what I’d like to tell people, but most of the time I just give them the logline (Again, if you want that answer, it’s at the bottom). I’m good at meeting deadlines. I’m good at making fun weird art projects. I’m bad at elevator pitches.

If you google me, my IMDB page is up near the top. That makes sense. I went to film school at NYU. I learned to be a cinematographer, and I exclusively did that until about 2013. I put all that work on a separate website, which you can visit here if you want. I love working on film sets. I still do it a lot, mostly because I live in Los Angeles and I like paying my rent, and frequently because one of my friends has a really amazing idea and asks me to help them make it a reality.

But that’s not really what I do any more. At least not exclusively. I think I went to film school because I was attracted to the concept of telling visual stories, but there are so many other mediums through which to tell stories that I couldn’t shake this feeling of being penned-in by the structure of the format and the overall resistance to change. And I couldn’t get over how much separation there was between the process of creating the work and the moment an audience finally got to experience the work.

Like a lot of kids that grew up in the 80s and 90s, I spent a lot of time in front of a computer. There was this collective dream that the future might finally be coming, and everyone would be a part of this awesome 8-bit metaverse. My family had a Mac, which made me think I was cooler than all the other nerds (relatively speaking). I learned some HyperTalk, then some C, sold some TI-83+ programs to my middle school friends and learned some HTML and JS once the internet became a thing, but I was never interested in being a professional Computer Scientist. Even as a kid I was pretty sure I wanted to spend my energy doing something that had to do with stories and images, and for the longest time CS just seemed like the opposite of that to me.

As I made my way through college, and my horizons broadened, I kept seeing more and more intersections between these two worlds. The overlapping area of the venn-diagram was getting bigger and bigger. I began coming across art, made with computers, that was actually really emotionally compelling. I started to research code as a tool for interactive storytelling and installation art. I was captivated by how the ethos of the open-source software community nurtured a thriving tech-art community all over the world. I got involved in art/tech or art/science hackathons, usually as a filmmaker or visual artist, while teaching myself Processing (the gateway drug of many, many artists way cooler than me).

I was part of a hackathon, a few years ago now, where we had to apply as a specific member of a team. I wasn’t sure if I should apply as a Storyteller or aTechnologist. At the bottom of the list they had another option, which seemed to be a pretty good fit - The Blackbox.

(Here there will be a cool 3D animation of a spinning black cube)

I think that that’s really the best distillation of what I do. I shape-shift to solve a problem. I learn a new skill or forget an old habit in service of a project. I feel just as comfortable loading 35mm film into a Panaflex as I do soldering a custom circuit board or debugging some of my hastily-written C++. I figure out what I need to learn or get better at so I can make what I want to make. I think the best answer to “what do you do?” should be describing my current project, no trying to come up with a way how all the projects fit together.

I know that’s still not a great answer to “what do you do?” but I’m confident that you are either somebody that totally gets it, or you stopped reading four paragraphs ago. If you’re the former, give me call. I think we can make really cool things together.
